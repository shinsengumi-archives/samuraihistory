To Tim Heaton
Not wishing to object to anyone's decided or destined
path through life, in terms of living the life of a
samurai, who are you to serve, who would be your
master (or perhaps mistress, this is the 21st
Century)? Devoid of a master, a samurai('one who
serves') became a ronin ('wave man') and as the
Tokugawa period evolved and the state demilitarized,
being a ronin often led to little more than crime,
debauchery, poverty, or jobs in teaching or medicine.
Good luck whatever happens
M.Lorimer

Hi, Timothy
I wasn't going to respond to your letter but the replies became so negative I decided to write.
What attracted me to study samurai history and culture was its philosophy or its at least its ideal of how to live life. It is nothing unique in this way of living; it can be found in the scriptures of all religions, the writings of sages from all over the world, and in the better self help books at your local bookstores. The difference is in the appearance.
I never thought to "become a samurai" though years ago there was that fasination. The closest I ever came was in my karate class where at one point my sensai had to explain to me that this was not the 17th Century. But from the books that I've read and my own understanding and practice of "The Way", the ideal living, it is all about attaining the higher self: being the best one can be in all endeavors, in all personal interactions, and to oneself.
No, you can't walk around the street with a katana (even if you take kendo, and if you do you will get bad vibes from your sensai, and possibly arrested by the police) but you could possibly learn tranquillity in a crazy world. Understand how to live because we all will die. To try to escape from life does more harm than good. We all have our duty as human beings to live here, right now, aware and awake.
Whether the samurai lived by these ideals or not does not matter.
"Each one of us has to create his own method. If you imitate you'll be wrong. you have to create for yourself" - Taisen Deshimaru.
OK, I have to go. I don't want to be late for work.
Take care of yourself, Tim.
