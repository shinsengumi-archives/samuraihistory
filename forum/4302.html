Re: [samuraihistory] geography question

by mijalo_jp

By the second half of the 12th century, it is hard to find places that the Taira were not present. Descended from a grandson of Emperor Kammu, Takamochi (governor of Kazusa), by 1180, the Taira/Heike possessed 30 of the 66 provinces through being zuryo, as well as possessing an alleged 500 shoen. After the successful defeat of Sutoku (the Hogen no ran, 1156), and Fujiwara no Nobuyori (the Heiji no ran, 1159), Taira no Kiyomori sat at the head of a vast network of provinces and shoen, throughout Honshu, but primarily concentrated in the west. He was not like a later daimyo centred upon a particular province, although it is clear that he considered Fukuhara on the Inland Sea as his base (for both poltical and Song-Chinese trade control), attempting to permenantly move the capital there in the late 1170s), but dominated positions and posts across the country; from regional officials, and estate owners, to family members and vassals within the North Gate bushidan.
M.Lorimer
