Re: [samuraihistory] Re: question?

by mijalo_kr

Daimyo relations
1. Between Toyotomi Hideyoshi and Tokugawa Ieyasu were
very strained at best . After the death of Oda
Nobunaga (1582), hideyoshi emerged as the next source
of authority, to the detriment od Shibata Katsuie and
Nobuo (Nobunaga's second son). Nobuo then turned to
Ieyasu for support . Only too willing to attack the
usurper (ie, Hideyoshi) Ieyasu joined Nobuo, and the
alliance led to the innconclusive Komaki Campaign of
May 1584 .
When Hideyoshi orchestrated the invasions of Korea, he
opted to leave the untrustworthy Ieyasu (and Date
Masamune) in Japan, rather than risking their presence
among a potentially-hostile (to the ailing
Hideyoshi!!)army of veteran samurai .
2. It's hard ti imagine Asai Nagamasa and Asai
Nagamasa were anything but enemies;
a. Shibata was a trusted-Oda loyalist, while Asai
supported the Asakura and the loose-Anti-Oda
alliance(rather than Nobunaga, his brother-in-law) in
the campaign that lead to defeat at Anegawa but
victory at Otsu (1570). in this campaign, Shibata
defended Chokoji-jo for Nobunaga against Rokkaku
forces .
b. As a result of the defeat of Nagamasa, Shibata was
given O-ichi and her three daughters by Nagamasa, but
this was the second time she'd been married to
Shibata, who had been forced to give her up (for
Nagamasa) as part of a Asai-Oda peace deal over Mino
province . Not exactly the basis for a wholesome
relationship between to\he two daimyo at the best of
times .
Hope this all helps
M.Lorimer

married to his (Samboshi's) cousin, Cha-cha (Oichi-no-Kata's daughter by
Asai), and knowing that Shibata was an Oda retainer and that Asai had
supported Asakura at Anegawa.
If my memory serves me correctly, it was the protection of Samboshi that
started the series of events that led -- ultimately -- to Shibata's
disastrous defeat at Shizugatake and his and Oichi-no-Kata's suicide at
Kita-no-Sho. (Of course, the blame for THAT defeat goes to one Sakuma
Morimasa, the general that disobeyed Shibata's orders to stop after his
defeat of Nakagawa Kiyohide. Although I cannot say that Shibata was exactly
himself honourable or without blame, as he was back at Kita-no-Sho and
nowhere NEAR the battle when it occurred.)
Now, when I go back to do the correction and rewriting process, I can
rewrite with that in mind, b/c the story takes place sometime between 1570
and 1573 (I'm not too sure right now, although as events unfold within the
writing, that will most certainly be determined), right during the time
Oichi-no-Kata was married to Asai. Part of the story already written
involves both Tokugawa and Toyotomi. (As the story I am working on is
fiction, Shibata -- who is, alas, one of my favourite figures in Japanese
history -- is, unfortunately, not a living presence or factor, although he
has been mentioned a couple of times so far.)
